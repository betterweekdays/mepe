History of Mary Ellen Pleasant

Source: the Paris Review

Mary Ellen Pleasant is a very successful 19th-century African American entrepreneur, financier, real estate magnate and abolitionist who identified herself as ‘a capitalist by profession’ in the 1890 United States Census. She came from humble beginnings being born into slavery, but would be later freed and work as an indentured servant. After moving to San Francisco, Pleasant developed a successful, 30-year business relationship with Thomas Bell (her last husband). Together, through smart investments and business dealings, they would amass a fortune estimated to be around 30 million dollars at the time. All the while, Pleasant continued her work in the abolitionist movement, and later after the emancipation, fighting for civil rights in California. She used her influence and wealth to employ Blacks (freed and runaways from the underground) at her various businesses.

Overview of Internship

The Gates Foundation, the Penn Center for Minority Serving Institutions (PENN CMSI), and The Whether have come together in an effort to increase future entrepreneurs from Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs) through the Mary Ellen Pleasant Entrepreneur (MEPE) Fellowship. It will highlight the characteristics that today’s young entrepreneurs need most: a keen intellect, a strong work ethic, and the desire to take risks in making the world a better place.

Gates, CMSI, and The Whether are offering each MEPE partner an opportunity to provide their student leaders the ability to apply to participate in a 10 week $2,500 virtual internship. Students will commit 4 hours a week (about 45 minutes a day) to the program. The MEPE Fellowship program is designed to enhance student’s leadership and entrepreneurship skills. Students will be interns with Better Weekdays, the company that owns The Whether. Their mission will be to build a mini-startup focused on student coaching using The Whether’s technology on campus. They will learn the fundamentals of distribution, the customer funnel, and taking risks through a 10-week curriculum.

25 fellowships will be awarded based on a combination of interest, on-campus leadership, academic achievement, and nominations from students’ HBCU advisors, professors, or administration. The selected fellows will also be invited to an awards dinner celebrating their success and networking with other entrepreneurial professionals on the University of Pennsylvania campus.
